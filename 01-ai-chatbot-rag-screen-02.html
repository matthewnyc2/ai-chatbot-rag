Quantum Entanglement & Error Correction

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where pairs or groups of particles are generated or interact in ways such that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the state of the others. In computing, this allows qubits to share information instantaneously across a system.

"Entanglement is the backbone of the 'Surface Code' in quantum error correction."

For error correction, entanglement is used to create **Logical Qubits** from several physical qubits. By entangling physical qubits, we can detect errors through 'syndrome measurements' without disturbing the encoded quantum information itself. This is essential because the act of observation typically destroys quantum states (decoherence).

It's a brilliant analogy. Much like **RAID 5 or 6** uses parity bits to reconstruct lost data from a failed drive, Quantum Error Correction (QEC) uses entanglement to distribute the information of one logical qubit across multiple physical ones.

However, the critical difference is the **No-Cloning Theorem**: we cannot simply copy quantum data. Instead, we use entanglement to create a 'distributed' state that can be repaired if one part of the system decoheres.
